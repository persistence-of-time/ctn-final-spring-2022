Social media is a key driver of misinformation within Latino communities

By: Anacaona Rodríguez Martinez, Lucy Cabrera y Gustavo Garcia

May 24, 2022
We join an effort to combat this problem. This is what we learned.
This story was originally published in Spanish.

Misinformation is shared in Spanish more easily on social networks than in other languages ​​for many reasons. For the past three months, we have joined an international fact-checking effort to combat disinformation shared in Spanish.

In March, we explained the difference between social media misinformation and disinformation, and how you can check whether your information is correct or false. We then share the stories of three Latinos, John Rodríguez, Jaime Longoria Castillo, and Grace Battaglia, who fight disinformation through their work.

So how do you combat misinformation shared in Spanish when community trust is lacking, and how was this group able to combat this?

The problem

Latinos are at risk of being victims of misinformation for a variety of factors. Whether they are about health or politics, the elements that facilitate their distribution are the same.

Professors Jeronimo Cortina and Brandon Rottinghaus analyzed how political behaviors in the Latin American community directly correlate with the impact of disinformation. They hypothesized that Latinos, while generally having weak partisan ties, have strong ideological beliefs. They added that, "exposure to selected political messages that resonate with people's beliefs shapes the depths of belief in conspiracy theories." This is more common when a conspiracy coincides with the ideological preferences of the individual and there is a lack of political knowledge.

A study conducted by Harvard concluded that non-white respondents, which included Latin Americans, taught less knowledge about COVID-19 and less media literacy than their white counterparts. Despite this, non-white respondents accepted COVID-19 misinformation at a higher rate. The study found an association between media literacy skills and knowledge of COVID-19, concluding that media literacy is essential for informing people of color with accurate information.

Cortina and Rottinghaus also said that Latinos who relied on social media as their primary source of information rather than traditional media were more likely to believe conspiracy theories. Social media is a key driver of disinformation because it increases party loyalty to the truth. In 2019, 52% of Latinos in the United States use Whatsapp. Cortina and Rottinghaus found that reports from the 2020 elections found that disinformation spread through Whatsapp, Telegram and Facebook raised fears of communism among other shared political fears in the Latin American community in the United States. People who rely on social media for news are more likely to believe and latch onto the misinformation spread on the platforms they use.

To provide Latin Americans with accurate information, access to fact-checking is critical. Latin American countries and Spain, according to journalists Victoria Moreno-Gil, Xavier Ramon and Ruth Rodríguez-Martínez, "share great challenges such as limited journalistic autonomy, difficulties in accessing public data, [and] the politicization of the media." This puts Latin Americans and Spaniards at risk of being victims of disinformation in the United States since they come with these customs from their countries of origin.

Through their research, Moreno-Gil, Ramón, and Rodríguez-Martínez analyzed a variety of small fact-checking organizations working in Latin America, including Chequeado in Argentina and Maldita.es in Spain.

At the forefront

All organizations evaluated identified three challenges in fact-checking: a difficulty accessing public data, limited resources for Spanish-speakers, and an inability to reach broader audiences. Despite this, their study concluded that these organizations have been able to make a positive contribution to spreading accurate information and debunking disinformation through the use of digital tools, including social media.

The lack of access to information for Latinos, whose often preferred language is Spanish, and the fast-paced nature of information dissipation on social media leads to the creation and spread of disinformation. To break this cycle, fact-checking that is accessible and conducted by those who understand the community is essential.

Where is the work taking place?

Small organizations are working across Latin America, the United States, and Spain to combat misinformation and disinformation across social media. These are just a few examples doing the fact-checking work:

MALDITA.ES

A non-profit medium, Maldita.es, purpose is to provide citizens with "tools so that they don't sneak up on you." Maldita Hemeroteca, Maldito Bulo, Maldita Ciencia and Maldito Dato focus on the control of disinformation and public discourse through fact-checking techniques and data journalism. We are a non-profit association with three main objectives:

  • Monitor and control political discourse and promote transparency in public and private institutions.
  • Verify and fight disinformation.
  • Promote media literacy and tech tools to create an aware community that can defend against disinformation as found in all areas.

ELDETECTOR

Considered the first fact-checking platform in Spanish in the United States. elDetector was born in March 2016 with the name of 'The lie detector', to fulfill the journalistic mission of seeking the truth and challenging public discourse, in the United States and Latin America. It belongs to the news division of Univision.

NEWTRAL

Project Newtral is first real-time fact-checking tool to fight against the fake news and disinformation

Newtral has recently finished an SME Instrument Phase 1 project, which was funded by the European Commission (€50’000 grant with a 70% reimbursement rate). Project objectives were:

  • Technical and Practical feasibility (SO1): We will review the state of art of i style="color: whitenvolved technologies to define an initial technical roadmap for an end-to-end solution for automated fact-checking.
  • Commercial and Business feasibility (SO2): Analysis of target markets, main stakeholders, customers and competitors in potential markets to identify potential partners, mostly active fact-checkers and reference customers or partners.
  • Financial feasibility (SO3): Adjustment and fine-tuning of business model. Adapting financial plan to target market segments, prices and operational costs. Revenue projections. Capital requirements, cost-benefit analysis, financing options and ROI. Elaboration of new business plan based on results.

LUPA

Since its inception, Lupa produces text, audio and video factchecks. It shares its verifications in journals, magazines, radios, websites, TV channels and social media - both inside and outside of Brazil. Its the first fact-checking agency in Brazil

ECUADOR CHEQUEA

Ecuador Chequea is a communication medium specialized in verification. In a world where the armies of disinformation abounds and is strong, the more containing walls, the better. Now we offer you two new sections: in "Al Grano" we will explain the whys, the hows and the wherefores of the most important news of the week; and in "In Depth" you will find our research.

COLOMBIA CHECK

Colombiacheck is a project of the "Editorial Board", a non-partisan, non-profit organization that brings together more than 100 associated journalists in Colombia to promote investigative journalism. The project consists of a digital, open and collaborative platform in which articles are published based on the technique of checking facts and data, which comes from a long tradition of Anglo-Saxon journalism and is known as fact-checking.

Colombiacheck is a signatory of the Global Network of Fact-checkers, IFCN, which certifies us as checkers that comply with the principles of said international community.

CHEQUEADO

Chequeado is the main project of the Fundación La Voz Pública. We are a non-partisan, non-profit digital media dedicated to verifying public discourse, fighting disinformation, promoting access to information, and opening up data.

We check the statements of politicians, economists, businessmen, public figures, the media and viral content on social networks and classify them from “true” to “false” according to their consistency with the facts and data to which they refer.

Verifying erroneous information is a daily challenge because something new appears every day that seeks to confuse people or institutions that seek their benefit by spreading falsehoods. However, we, as fact-checkers, seek to be transparent with the evidence that shows what is true and false, in an empathetic way so that our audience learns to differentiate when news comes from a false medium.

What is missing now is fixing the difficulty of accessing public data, making resources more accessible to Spanish-speakers, and reaching broader audiences.

Getting the audience involved in combating disinformation is the main key to combating the problem. They can do this by reading the verifications from any of the verification agencies that do their work in Spanish, such as elDetector (USA), Maldita.es (Spain), Chequeado (Argentina), and more. Also, they can make sure not to share links that carry the disinformation they find on social networks, or send it to these groups to verify if it is false.
