1. BLOCKS (Stamps)

The printmaking process begins by carving blocks or stamps out of sheets of plywood. Mr. Nutini uses a CNC router to create these blocks efficiently ahead of time and has developed a library of blocks that he uses throughout his print projects.

2. INKING

Once the blocks are selected to be included into a composition, they are moved to the inking area. Here they get an even layer of an oil based ink applied with a roller or brayer. The colors are carefully selected ahead of time and layered in a way that will make sense in the final composition.

3. PRESS

After the block gets inked they are placed on the bed of the relief press. These presses have been in use for centuries, and the technology has not changed very much over the decades. Master printers have used these presses to print etchings, woodcuts, colographs, and other types of prints.

At the press, a sheet of paper gets placed on top of the plate and is run under a cylinder that applies even pressure as the plate passes through the press. This process gets repeated each time a new color is applied. Sometimes prints have over 10 to 15 layers of color once they are completed.

4. DRYING

The prints get placed in the drying rack after they have been run through the press. Depending on the density of the color it takes several days for the ink to dry because the ink used in the process is oil based and it takes several days for the binder in the ink to oxidize. This is where they completely dry at the end of the printing process.

5. STAGING WALL

Here is where the dried prints that are awaiting further colors are staged, or where the final pieces are inspected. This space is very important because it allows the master printer to review and compare different variations of the prints to control for quality and consistency.

6. FINAL INSPECTION & EDITIONING

During the final inspection, the prints are curated for quality and consistency. Prints that were damaged during the printing process are taken out of the final edition. At this stage the total number of prints in the edition is determined. The prints are individually numbered, titled, and signed by the artist.
